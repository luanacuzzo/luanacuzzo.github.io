OVERVIEW

Exploring better ways to book a flight online has come a long way, yet people still struggle in the process. Motivated by the potential to create a friction-free booking process, I analyzed what giant competitors in this industry are doing to offer a better experience. Then, I suggested a solution to simplify the way we book flights online.

Simplifying that process means primarily reducing user bugs and frictions. However, focusing only on taking out the pain doesn't seem enough to me. What if we could bring more pleasure and innovation into the experience? Let's start with simple questions. Why do people drop along the way? How can we speed up the process? How can we make it a more human-like experience?

The Problem

Why are airlines suffering with low conversion rates on their mobile apps?

It all starts with users looking forward to going on holiday in a couple of weeks. They access their favorite airline apps to research prices and availability, which was supposed to be a simple step-by-step task. As it turns out, users start coming across inconsistencies and misleading pieces of information. They get more and more confused with different fare types. And finally, users don't feel compelled to proceed to checkout.They are much better off abandoning the process right there.

Exploring Solutions

Building a more engaging app that not only allows for discovery and inspiration but provides a simple search function that is based on user's needs.

IDEATION

Affinity Diagram

After making a simple Afinnity Diagram to compile and make sense of all reserach data, I identified the problems that are leading people to make mistakes and causing them to drop along the way, then I decided what pain points to focus on:

Choosing a Fare Type is Problematic

Users are so confuse that they choose a fare-type like they are already making a mistake, like that's the end of the line for them.

Unclear Filtering Options

Airlines are not using real-world language and some choices are redundunt. Plus, the filters are not visible.

Difficulty Selecting Seats

Who am I selecting this seat for? Is this the departing or returning flight? Do I have to pay extra?

No Personalization Whatsoever

Airline apps suggest (or try to push) a fare type based on... well, nothing. Even worse, they don't tell users what the differences are right away.

DEFINING

Design Decisions and Rationale

Mobile First Approach

It's time we enabled users with the flexibility and security to book a flight from anywhere with a simple reach into their pockets.

Filters

To replace long texts and give users control from the start.

Let's dismistify the fair-type confusion

Airlines tend to force 'the best' or 'recommended' fare to users without taking into account what their real needs are.

Let's give users an opportunity to upgrade their flight experience with the items they choose only. And let's allow them to do that before and after booking the flight!

No more fooling users around with hidden stopovers!

Misleading information was identified to be one of the main reasons why users abandon the booking process when proceeding to checkout.

Let's make things clear for uses right from the start. If we don't tell users about stopovers at this stage, they will forget all about it later in the process.

Using a conversational language not only will keep users engaged through the process but will help them make decisions, it will also eliminate confusion and raise their trust in the app.

Learnings

#1 Reliability is key for a solid Airline UX

It's very frustrating for users to go through a whole booking process, only to find out at the end that they are paying for something they didn't ask for. Users won't forgive airlines that hide important information such as stopovers during the booking process. Finding out about stopovers only at the checkout stage, will really make users abandon the process. As a result of their frustration, users won't feel inclined to ever try that same airline again.

#2 Personalization saves scrolling time

Why give users a thousand options they don't need, when we can give them control of their preferences right form the start? Even though personalization adds up some time to the booking process, it saves scrolling time. It's more frustrating to go through a thousand of different flight options then to answer a couple of questions that are pertnent to users'real needs.

#3 Just the right number of filters give users control

Users should be given a positive feeling of choice and control when having to decide among so many different options, and that should be done in the beggning of the process. Users need to understand what their options are and why they have these options.

#4 Innovation Require Small Steps

Taking into account that, even with lots of bumps, most users are able to accomplish the online booking task, we can consider the Airline UX relatively satisfactory. However, there is still a lot of room for improvement and innovation. Obviously, it can't be done overnight, because user's mental models are not ready for sudden big changes. The least we can do though to bring a more human-like feel to Airlines apps is to change language with one that is more conversational. Then, we should show more interest in what it is that users are actually looking for. Forcing them to choose a fare-type that the Airline wants hasn't worked.

Imagining the future of Airline Websites, I can picture a platform that is responsive, as they shoulbe be reliabe and consistent regardless of what device users want to book their flights from. I picture a platform that is smart as to know where users are booking a flight from, and what their preferences are based on history.

Travelers are become more modern and Airline apps should follow along.
